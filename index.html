Our Story

Sept. 2010

A mutual friend - Rachel Heaton, aka Blade, aka Rayban, aka “bad Rachel” - invites Rachel to go to a book release party. The book is about Ruby, and Rachel thinks, “I love fine minerals!” She wonders why the author, Paul, pays so much attention to her at his party. The next day, they become Facebook friends.

Fall & Winter 2010

Paul and Rachel hang out. Rachel drags Paul out to see “Case 39” and to the ice-skating rink; Paul teaches her about NYC karaoke, bodegas and sweet drinking spots. They go bowling and tie two games in a row (scores: 117 and 142). Paul makes a Brazilian feast for Rachel (farofa with bananas, fried yuca with habaneros and white fish with coconut sauce) - the first time he’s ever cooked for lady friend!

March 2011

Paul decides to woo Rachel on a spring break trip. Rachel chooses St. Lucia because it’s a new country and has interesting rock formations. They are both nervous about actually spending more than a few hours together at one time. Things work out: they are upgraded to the honeymoon suite and discover a mutual love of 90s music.

July 2011

Paul introduces Rachel as “his girlfriend” to someone they run into at a baseball game. How presumptuous! But Rachel does not protest.

July 2011

Rachel visits Denver for the first time, where she meets Paul’s family and friends, sees his old high school and the site of his first tech-related job (Computer City!), and samples green chili and bison meat.

September 2011

Paul and Rachel move in together in Williamsburg, Brooklyn. Paul becomes the adoptive father of Rachel’s cat, Kit, aka, the Small Orange Baby. They celebrate with a hipster housewarming party.

May 2012

Rachel brings Paul to Puerto Rico for his birthday. They take a terrifying single-prop, eight-seat flight to the tiny island of Culebra, where they see a sea turtle while snorkeling and meet cool, mangrove-painting locals.

September 2012

They venture to Asia for two weeks. Together, they stalk Komodo dragons and bathe out of a bucket in rural Indonesia. Rachel acquires a large scar after flying over her handle bars whilemountain biking in Hong Kong, and Paul gets food poisoning at the airport in Malaysia.

January 2013

Paul drags Rachel across the country for a three-month stint in San Francisco, where he takes part in a startup accelerator called Y Combinator. The Small Orange Baby comes along as Rachel’s psychiatrist-certified “comfort animal” - to get around their new apartment's no-pet regulations.

July 2013

On a two-week trip to Sweden and Norway, they explore a Soviet ghost town in Svalbard, drink under the midnight sun and eat at one of the world’s best restaurants. Scandinavia, they learn, is wildly expensive.

September 2013

Paul takes Rachel to Bermuda “for her birthday.” He gets horribly seasick on a morning scuba diving excursion, and misses the second dive because his ears won't pop. Undeterred, that evening he proposes to Rachel on their hotel balcony at sunset, with champers! She says yes.

March 2014

They get a civil union so Rachel can piggyback on Paul’s health insurance policy. Romantic!

August 2014

They ride two-humped camels together in the Gobi… This is happening right now.

September 2014

They launch this website, and prepare to throw an amazing party for their family and friends!

Bride & Groom

Rachel

Rachel grew up exploring the bayous and beaches of her native Biloxi, Mississippi. Friends often referred to her house as “Noah’s Ark” because of the menagerie of animals found there - chickens, iguanas, hedgehogs, snakes… She and her sister could often be found trapping feral kittens under a derelict bridge, which they tamed and found homes for.

Her love of all thing wild and green led her to study biology at Loyola University New Orleans. After graduating, she headed to Japan for a year to teach English, and then enrolled in an ecology master’s program in Europe. She spent time researching illegal wildlife trade in a peat swamp in Vietnam, where she ate a rat, got attacked by thousands of bees and was thrown off a motorbike. But mid-way through her degree she realized that statistics and data collection weren’t her favorite things, so she applied to NYU’s science journalism master’s program. Writing about science rather than doing science turned out to be a perfect fit - as was New York City. Now, she contributes to outlets such as The New York Times, the BBC and Smithsonian.

When not hunched over her desk rushing to meet a deadline, Rachel loves traveling (she’s been to 40 countries), cooking (she does it to spoil Paul) and taking photos (oftentimes of food, while traveling!).

Paul

Paul hails from Denver, Colorado, where he grew up skiing, skating and playing video games. His obsession with computers surfaced early, after his grandmother gave him a Commodore 64c for his 9th birthday. Hooked, he wrote his first computer program in 4th grade.

After high school, Paul worked as a PC technician and network administrator in Denver, and as a aoftware tester at Microsoft in Redmond, Washington. After returning to Denver, he worked as a programmer at a startup, and later at Air Force Space Command. He moved to New York in 2005 to live in the city of his dreams and to attend Columbia University. He pursued a degree in Computer Science, studying artificial intelligence and machine learning. While in school, Paul also worked on projects for the New York Jets, BBC, Channel 4 UK and Google.

Since finishing school, Paul has become an active member of the NYC tech community. In 2009, he founded the New York City Machine Learning Meetup, which now has over 5,200 members. He is the author of Service Oriented Design with Ruby and Rails, and is the editor for Addison Wesley's book and video series Data & Analytics. In 2013, he participated in the startup accelerator Y Combinator and founded the company InfluxDB. He is a frequent speaker at conferences and user groups.

Frequently Asked Questions

What about transportation?

Cabs from the airport into town cost about $30. New Orleans does not have very good public transportation, however, so if you plan to do a lot of exploring it might be worth renting a car.

Can I bring my kids to the wedding?

Unfortunately, the wedding venue does not allow children under the age of 10 (except those standing in the wedding). Babysitters can be hired through Dependable Kid Care or Care.com.

Can I bring a hot date or my bff as a plus one?

Unfortunately, our venue has very limited space. But please email us and we can discuss.

Is New Orleans Dangerous?

The Garden District is quite safe, but some parts of the city - including the fringes of the French Quarter - can be a bit sketchy after dark. If you’re out late, try to travel as a group, and avoid parking on questionable-looking side streets or vacant lots. Better to pay for parking in one of the secure lots, just to be safe.

NOLA

If you have never been to New Orleans or the Deep South, we encourage you to build in extra time for exploring! The South is culturally distinct from other parts of the country, and there no place in the world quite like New Orleans. From bayous to beaches, historic plantations to century-old cemeteries, and funk jazz to creole cooking, there is much to discover.

To help you make the most of your trip (and to avoid the tourists traps!), we’ve put together a few of our favorite sites, tastes and sips in New Orleans, as well as in the greater South.

Tours & Classes

There are lots of entertaining, informative walking tours around the city, all of which contain a significant dose of New Orleans history and culture. Here are some options:

  • Haunted History: French Quarter ghost, cemetery, voodoo, scandal, vampire and Garden district ghosts and legends tours.
  • Magic Tours: tours “for the intelligent traveller,” delivered by professors, historians and journalists. Ghost and voodoo, St. Louis cemetery, Garden District and Lafayette cemetery, and French Quarter tours.
  • Friends of the Cabildo: French Quarter walking tours with an emphasis on history, architecture and folklore. Tours include free admission to the 1850 House Museum.
  • Historic New Orleans Tours: Usual assortment of ghosts, vamps, etc, plus an adults only “Scandalous Cocktail Hour Tour,” which delves into hookers, crime and booze.

There are swamp tours that are a short drive from New Orleans. You’ll see alligators (potentially some up to ten-feet long or so!), decades-old cypress trees, Cajun floating homes and more. Well worth the trip, even if you’re not a nature person or whatever. If you don’t have your own car, they can pick you up from your hotel. Lots of options for those but I think Honey Island Swamp Tours are the best.

Mardi Gras tours are arranged through Mardi Gras World. They have “a magical float den” on site. There’s another tour in the Treme neighborhood that focuses on the Mardi Gras Indians and black Mardi Gras.

Alternatively, if you’re interested in learning about the lasting legacy of Hurricane Katrina while also getting a little excercise, check out the Ninth Ward Rebirth Bike Tours. Additionally, there’s a green home you can visit in the Lower 9th Ward called the Global Green House. There are also bus tours that cover more distance and paint a fuller picture of the Hurricane’s impact on the city and surrounding parishes.

For Cajun, Creole and Southern cooking classes, check out the New Orleans School of Cooking, Langlois Culinary Crossroads and the New Orleans Cooking Experience.

You can take two hour tours up and down the Mississippi River on the historic Natchez Steam Boat, with dinner and lunch options, and jazz played on all trips. There’s also jazz dinner cruises and Civil War battle field trips on the historic Creole Queen paddleboat.

Finally, if you don’t have a car but want to check out some plantations, the Old River Road Plantation Adventure hits four plantations and has a swamp add-on option. They’ll pick you up by car or you can take their paddle boat.

Bay St. Louis Cute historic Gulf town that was destroyed by Katrina. Cafes and shops have since reopened, and there's a quiet beach. Bay St. Louis, Mississippi
Ohr O’Keefe Museum of Art See the work of "the mad potter of Biloxi." Biloxi, Mississippi
Maritime and Seafood Industry Museum Learn about the Gulf Coast's historic seafood industry. This museum was washed away by Katrina but recently reopened. Biloxi, Mississippi
Beauvoir Jefferson Davis' final home, with a museum and historic cemetery on site. Biloxi, Mississippi
Shrimp boat ride Climb aboard a shrimping boat for a hands-on demonstration and boat ride. Biloxi, Mississippi
Schooner ride Take a ride on the historic Biloxi Schooner. Biloxi, Mississippi
Ocean Springs Cute shops, cafes, bars, restaurants. Ocean Springs, Mississippi
Walter Anderson Museum of Art See works of "an American master," who depicted nature in striking, abstract watercolors. Ocean Springs, Mississippi

Eating & Entertainment

Abita Brewing Company Visit Abita beer brewery for a tour or tasting. Covington, Louisiana
Tabasco Sauce Factory Take a tour of the Tabasco Sauce factory, which also includes a 170-acre nature area you can explore. Avery Island, Louisiana
Dong Phuong Excellent Vietnamese restaurant and bakery in the heart of "Little Saigon," where many of New Orleans' Vietnamese residents live. Buddhist temples, grocery stores and karaoke parlors abound nearby. Also one of the areas obliterated by Katrina; damage is still readily apparent. New Orleans East
Daiquiris Now Drive-through daiquiri shop. Comes in gallons. Slidell, Louisiana
Casinos The Mississippi Gulf Coast is the eighth largest casino hub in the US. Some good options include the Beau Rivage, the Hard Rock and the Imperial Palace. Gulfport and Biloxi, Mississippi
The Shed Barbeque and Blues Joint Excellent bbq and live music. Ocean Springs, Mississippi
What the Pho Best Vietnamese food on the Gulf Coast. D’Iberville, Mississippi
Shaw’s Fish House Best fried catfish and hush puppies in the South. Perkinston, Mississippi

Nature

Honey Island Swamp Gorgeous cypress swamp, with fantastic tours. (Pickup available in the city.) Slidell, Louisiana
Jean Lafitte Swamp Boardwalk and trails meander through this nature reserve. Marrero, Louisiana
Insta-Gator Ranch and Hatchery Alligator farm. Kids (or adults!) can play with baby gators, and there are educational tours. Marrero, Louisiana
Global Wildlife Center Feed and pet wildebeest, zebra, giraffes and more on a safari ride. Great for the kiddies. Folsom, Louisiana
Lake Martin Swampy lake and birding paradise. "Pack and Paddle" in Lafayette can arrange paddle excursions. Lake Martin, Louisiana
Liuzza Produce Farm Strawberry picking & petting zoo. Good for kids. Amite, Louisiana
Ship Island Barrier island along the Mississippi Gulf Coast. See dolphins, horseshoe crabs and other wildlife. There's a Civil War fort on the island that's open to the public. Umbrella and chair rentals are available. Boats run daily. Gulfport, Mississippi
Pascagoula River Take a boat tour of the Pascagoula River - the only continuously free-flowing, non-altered river in the US - and McCoy's Swamp, arranged by the Pascagoula Audubon Center. Pascagoula, Mississippi
Clark Creek Natural Area Hike to Mississippi's only waterfall. Woodville, Mississippi
Gulf Shores Beautiful beaches without much development; plenty of nature and deserted stretches of sand and dunes. There's a fort here, too. Gulf Shores, Alabama
